** Forms
There are three main forms of a quadratic expression or equation.
All of them represent the same parabola, just written in different ways.

A quadratic is any expression of the form:

\[
ax^2+bx+c
\]

where a â‰  0.

A special case occurs when a = 1:

\[
x^2+bx+c
\]

This is called a monic quadratic.
Many basic factoring problems use this simpler form.

-----------------------------------------------------
1. Standard form
This is the most common and general form.

\[
f(x)=ax^2+bx+c
\]

a controls the opening direction and vertical stretch.
b affects the axis of symmetry and horizontal placement.
c is the y-intercept, where the graph crosses the y-axis.

Example:

\[
f(x)=2x^2+3x-5
\]

Special monic case of standard form:

\[
f(x)=x^2+7x+10
\]

Here the leading coefficient is 1.

-----------------------------------------------------
2. Factored form (intercept form)
This form shows the roots (x-intercepts) directly.

\[
f(x)=a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)
\]

r_1 and r_2 are the solutions to the equation.
The graph crosses the x-axis at these values.

Example:

\[
f(x)=(x-2)(x+5)
\]

Roots:

\[
x=2,\quad x=-5
\]

If the leading coefficient is not 1:

\[
f(x)=2(x-2)(x+5)
\]

-----------------------------------------------------
3. Vertex form
This form shows the vertex of the parabola directly.

\[
f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k
\]

(h,k) is the vertex of the parabola.
a controls the opening direction and vertical stretch.

Example:

\[
f(x)=2(x-3)^2+1
\]

Vertex:

\[
(3,1)
\]

-----------------------------------------------------
Summary

General standard form:

\[
ax^2+bx+c
\]

Special monic standard form:

\[
x^2+bx+c
\]

Factored form:

\[
a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)
\]

Vertex form:

\[
a(x-h)^2+k
\]


** Factoring Quadratics
Starting form

A quadratic written like:

\[
x^2+ax+b=0
\]

is a monic quadratic in standard form.

From this form, it is not obvious where the graph crosses the x-axis or what values of \(x\) make the expression equal to zero.

-----------------------------------------------------
After factoring

If we rewrite the left side as:

\[
(x+m)(x+n)=0
\]

the equation is now in factored form.

-----------------------------------------------------
Why this is useful

1. It makes solving the equation easy.

If:

\[
(x+m)(x+n)=0
\]

then one of the factors must be zero.
This follows from the zero product property.

If:

\[
ab=0
\]

then:

\[
a=0 
\]
Or
\[
b=0 
\]

Apply this to the quadratic:

\[
x+m=0 \quad \text{or} \quad x+n=0
\]

Solve each equation:

\[
x=-m \quad \text{or} \quad x=-n
\]

So the solutions appear immediately.
